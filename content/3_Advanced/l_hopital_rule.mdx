# Introduction
 
L'Hôpital's Rule is an important technique that makes our lives easier when dealing with certain forms of tricky limits — those that evaluate to **indeterminate form**.
 
# Identifying Indeterminate Form
 
## Indeterminate Form
 
Indeterminate form arises when evaluating a limit leads to an expression that cannot be truly evaluated. There are several different kinds:
 
**Quotient Forms** are the most common:
 
- $\dfrac{0}{0}$
- $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$
**Other Forms** include:
 
- **Product Form:** $0 \cdot \infty$ (e.g. $\lim_{x \to 0^+} x \ln x$)
- **Difference Form:** $\infty - \infty$ (e.g. $\lim_{x \to 0^+} \dfrac{1}{x^2} - \dfrac{1}{\tan x}$)
- **Power Form:** $\infty^0$, $0^0$, or $1^\infty$ (e.g. $\lim_{x \to \infty} x^{1/x}$)
## Getting to Quotient Form
 
L'Hôpital's Rule requires the limit to be in quotient form. Non-quotient indeterminate forms must first be algebraically rearranged into a fraction — the examples below illustrate how.
 
# Applying L'Hôpital's Rule
 
Once a limit is confirmed to be in indeterminate quotient form, L'Hôpital's Rule states:
 
If $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}$ evaluates to $\dfrac{0}{0}$ or $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$, then:
 
$$
\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}
$$
 
If the result is still indeterminate, the rule can be applied **repeatedly**.
 
<Info title="Important">
L'Hôpital's Rule **only applies** when the limit is in indeterminate form. Applying it to a non-indeterminate limit will give an incorrect result.
 
</Info>
## Worked Examples
 
> **Example 1:** Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin(x)}{x}$.
>
> **Solution:** Both $\sin(x)$ and $x$ approach $0$ as $x \to 0$, giving the form $\dfrac{0}{0}$. Setting $f(x) = \sin(x)$ and $g(x) = x$, we apply L'Hôpital's Rule:
>
> $$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin(x)}{x} = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\cos(x)}{1} = \boxed{1}$$
 
> **Example 2:** Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x + 5}{2x + 1}$.
>
> **Solution:** As $x \to \infty$, both numerator and denominator grow without bound, giving $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$. Applying L'Hôpital's Rule:
>
> $$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x + 5}{2x + 1} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3}{2} = \boxed{\frac{3}{2}}$$
 
> **Example 3:** Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0^+} x \ln x$.
>
> **Solution:** As $x \to 0^+$, we have $x \to 0$ and $\ln x \to -\infty$, giving the form $0 \cdot \infty$. Rewrite as a quotient by dividing instead of multiplying:
>
> $$\lim_{x \to 0^+} x \ln x = \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\ln x}{1/x}$$
>
> Now apply L'Hôpital's Rule (this is $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$):
>
> $$= \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1/x}{-1/x^2} = \lim_{x \to 0^+} (-x) = \boxed{0}$$
 
> **Example 4:** Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0^+} \left(\frac{1}{x^2} - \frac{1}{\tan x}\right)$.
>
> **Solution:** As $x \to 0^+$, both terms approach $\infty$, giving the form $\infty - \infty$. Combine into a single fraction by cross-multiplying:
>
> $$\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\tan(x) - x^2}{x^2 \tan(x)}$$
>
> Apply L'Hôpital's Rule:
>
> $$= \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{\sec^2(x) - 2x}{x^2 \sec^2(x) + 2x\tan(x)}$$
>
> As $x \to 0^+$: the numerator approaches $\sec^2(0) = 1$, while the denominator approaches $0$. Hence the limit is $\boxed{\infty}$.
 
> **Example 5:** Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} x^{1/x}$.
>
> **Solution:** This has the form $\infty^0$. Take the natural log to convert to a quotient:
>
> $$\ln\!\left(x^{1/x}\right) = \frac{\ln x}{x}$$
>
> Using the continuity of $\ln$:
>
> $$\ln\!\left(\lim_{x \to \infty} x^{1/x}\right) = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln x}{x}$$
>
> Apply L'Hôpital's Rule (this is $\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$):
>
> $$= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1/x}{1} = 0$$
>
> Therefore $\ln\!\left(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} x^{1/x}\right) = 0$, which gives $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} x^{1/x} = e^0 = \boxed{1}$.
 
# Proof
 
Starting with a quotient limit that evaluates to $\dfrac{0}{0}$, we know $f(a) = g(a) = 0$:
 
$$
\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{g(x) - g(a)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{\dfrac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a}}{\dfrac{g(x) - g(a)}{x - a}}
$$
 
Since the limit of a quotient equals the quotient of the limits (when both exist):
 
$$
= \frac{\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a}}{\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} \frac{g(x) - g(a)}{x - a}} = \frac{f'(a)}{g'(a)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}
$$
 
The numerator and denominator are precisely the difference quotient definitions of $f'$ and $g'$.
 
# Shortcomings
 
L'Hôpital's Rule **only works** when the limit is in indeterminate form. Misapplying it to a determinate limit yields a wrong answer.
 
> **Example (misuse):** Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x^2 + 5}{3x + 4}$.
>
> **Correct approach:** Substituting $x = 1$ directly: $\dfrac{1 + 5}{3 + 4} = \dfrac{6}{7}$.
>
> **Incorrect use of L'Hôpital's Rule:** Since this is *not* indeterminate form, applying the rule gives $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 1} \frac{2x}{3} = \frac{2}{3}$, which is **wrong**. Always check for indeterminate form before applying the rule.
