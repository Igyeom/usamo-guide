# Chain Rule
 
How do we take the derivative of $\sin(3x)$? We know how to take the derivative of $\sin(x)$, so it may seem like $\sin(3x)$ would just be $\cos(3x)$ — but it's not. Instead, we have to apply the chain rule. The chain rule is a simple yet incredibly important rule in calculus that allows us to take the derivative of more advanced functions more easily.
 
At its most basic level, the **chain rule** states that if:
 
$$
f(x) = g(h(x))
$$
 
then:
 
$$
f'(x) = \frac{df(x)}{dx} = g'(h(x))h'(x)
$$
 
In words, this means that the derivative of a function $f$ that equals $g$ of $h$ of $x$, where $h$ and $g$ are any functions, equals $g'$ evaluated at $h(x)$, multiplied by $h'(x)$. This may initially seem strange or abstract, and makes more sense with an example.
 
> **Example 1:** What is the derivative of $\sin(3x)$?
>
> **Solution:** Let $f(x) = \sin(3x) = g(h(x))$ where $g(x) = \sin(x)$ and $h(x) = 3x$. Then:
>
> $$f'(x) = \frac{df(x)}{dx} = g'(h(x))h'(x) = \cos(3x) \cdot 3 = \boxed{3\cos(3x)}$$
>
> since the derivative of $\sin$ is $\cos$, and the derivative of $3x$ is $3$.
 
This principle can be extended to even more complex functions, as in the example below.
 
> **Example 2:** What is the derivative of $\sin(\sin(\sin(x)))$?
>
> **Solution:** Let $f(x) = \sin(\sin(\sin(x))) = g(h(x))$ where:
>
> $$g(x) = \sin(x), \quad h(x) = p(q(x)) = \sin(\sin(x)), \quad p(x) = \sin(x), \quad q(x) = \sin(x)$$
>
> Then, applying the chain rule repeatedly:
>
> $$f'(x) = g'(h(x)) \cdot h'(x) = \cos(\sin(\sin(x))) \cdot h'(x)$$
>
> $$= \cos(\sin(\sin(x))) \cdot p'(q(x)) \cdot q'(x)$$
>
> $$= \boxed{\cos(\sin(\sin(x))) \cdot \cos(\sin(x)) \cdot \cos(x)}$$
 
## Proof of the Chain Rule
 
We can express the derivative of $f(g(x))$ as:
 
$$
(f(g(x)))' = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(g(x)) - f(g(a))}{x - a}
$$
 
Noting that $g(x) \neq g(a)$ for $x$ near $a$ (unless the function is a horizontal line, in which case the chain rule is unnecessary):
 
$$
= \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(g(x)) - f(g(a))}{g(x) - g(a)} \cdot \frac{g(x) - g(a)}{x - a}
$$
 
Since the limit of a product is the product of the limits, and using the definition of a derivative, this equals the familiar result $f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$.
 
## Practice Problems
 
> **Example 3:** A rocket has the position function $r(t) = 5t + 0.5\sin(3t^2 + 4t)$. What is its velocity?
>
> **Solution:** Taking the derivative using the chain rule:
>
> $$\boxed{v(t) = 5 + 0.5\cos(3t^2 + 4t)(6t + 4)}$$
 
Take the derivative of the following functions.
 
> **Problem 1:** $4(5x + 34)^2$
>
> **Answer:** $8(5x + 34) \cdot 5$
 
> **Problem 2:** $5(4x^2 + 6x + 38)^2$
>
> **Answer:** $10(4x^2 + 6x + 38)(8x + 6)$
 
> **Problem 3:** $6(2x + 3)^3$
>
> **Answer:** $18(2x + 3)^2 \cdot 2$
 
> **Problem 4:** $\sin(4x^2 + 3)$
>
> **Answer:** $\cos(4x^2 + 3) \cdot 8x$
 
> **Problem 5:** $\tan(3x + 2)$
>
> **Answer:** $\sec^2(3x + 2) \cdot 3$
 
> **Problem 6:** $\left(\dfrac{4x - 3}{2x + 1}\right)^2$
>
> **Answer:** $2\left(\dfrac{4x-3}{2x+1}\right)\left(\dfrac{(2x+1)(4) - (4x-3)(2)}{(2x+1)^2}\right)$
 
> **Problem 7:** $\sin(\cos(4x^2))$
>
> **Answer:** $\cos(\cos(4x^2)) \cdot (-\sin(4x^2)) \cdot 8x$
 
> **Problem 8:** $\sin(10x)$
>
> **Answer:** $\cos(10x) \cdot 10$
 
> **Problem 9:** $\tan(3x^2 + 4x)$
>
> **Answer:** $\sec^2(3x^2 + 4x) \cdot (6x + 4)$
 
> **Problem 10:** $3e^{3x}$
>
> **Answer:** $3e^{3x} \cdot 3 = 9e^{3x}$
 
> **Problem 11:** $5e^{16x^2 + 4x}$
>
> **Answer:** $5e^{16x^2 + 4x}(32x + 4)$
 
> **Problem 12:** $\sin\!\left(5e^{16x^2 + 4x}\right)$
>
> **Answer:** $\cos\!\left(5e^{16x^2+4x}\right) \cdot 5e^{16x^2+4x} \cdot (32x + 4)$
 
> **Problem 13:** $\sin(\cos(\sin(\cos(10x^2 + 1))))$
>
> **Answer:** $\cos(\cos(\sin(\cos(10x^2+1)))) \cdot (-\sin(\sin(\cos(10x^2+1)))) \cdot \cos(\cos(10x^2+1)) \cdot (-\sin(10x^2+1)) \cdot 20x$
