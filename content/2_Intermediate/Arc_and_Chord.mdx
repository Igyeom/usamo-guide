## Overview

Arcs and chords are closely connected in circle geometry. A central angle determines both the arc it intercepts and the chord connecting the endpoints of that arc.

Many AMC geometry problems can be solved by moving freely between angles, arcs, and chords. In particular, equal chords correspond to equal arcs, and larger arcs correspond to longer chords.

Note: The little triangle over the names of segments notates an arc. For example, with arcs AB and CD,

$$
\widehat{AB}=\widehat{CD},
$$


## Definitions and Key Ideas

A chord is a segment whose endpoints lie on a circle.

An arc is a portion of the circle between two points on the circle.

If a central angle intercepts an arc of measure $\theta^\circ$, then the arc itself also has measure $\theta^\circ$.

For example, if

$$
\angle AOB=80^\circ,
$$

where $O$ is the center of the circle, then arc $AB$ also measures

$$
80^\circ.
$$

The central angle, intercepted arc, and corresponding chord are all related.

## Fundamental Relationships

### Equal Chords and Equal Arcs

One of the most important facts in circle geometry is:

* Equal chords subtend equal arcs.
* Equal arcs subtend equal chords.
* Equal chords subtend equal central angles.

Thus if

$$
AB=CD,
$$

then

$$
\widehat{AB}=\widehat{CD},
$$

and the corresponding central angles are equal.

### Larger Arcs Correspond to Larger Chords

If one arc has greater measure than another, then its corresponding chord is longer.

For example, if

$$
m(\widehat{AB})>m(\widehat{CD}),
$$

then

$$
AB>CD.
$$

This fact is often useful in comparison problems.

### Diameter and Semicircles

A diameter divides a circle into two semicircles.

Since a full circle measures

$$
360^\circ,
$$

each semicircle measures

$$
180^\circ.
$$

Any chord whose endpoints are opposite ends of a diameter is the longest possible chord in the circle.

## Arc Length

Arc measure and arc length are different ideas.

* Arc measure is measured in degrees.
* Arc length is measured in units of distance.

If a circle has radius $r$ and central angle $\theta$ measured in radians, then the arc length is

$$
s=r\theta.
$$

When using this formula, always convert degrees to radians first.

For example,

$$
60^\circ=\frac{\pi}{3}.
$$

## Chords and Symmetry

Many circle problems become easier by looking for symmetry.

If a radius is drawn perpendicular to a chord, then it bisects the chord.

Conversely, if a radius bisects a chord, then it is perpendicular to the chord.

This gives a useful theorem:

A radius perpendicular to a chord cuts the chord into two equal pieces.

This fact frequently appears in AMC 8 and AMC 10 geometry problems.

## Worked Example

A circle has radius $10$ and central angle $60^\circ$. Find the chord length.

Since the central angle is $60^\circ$, the corresponding triangle formed by the two radii and the chord has side lengths

$$
10,\quad10,\quad10.
$$

Therefore the triangle is equilateral, and the chord length is

$$
\boxed{10}.
$$

## More Examples

### Example 1: Arc Length

Find the arc length when

$$
r=6
$$

and

$$
\theta=150^\circ.
$$

Convert to radians:

$$
150^\circ=\frac{5\pi}{6}.
$$

Then

$$
s=r\theta=6\left(\frac{5\pi}{6}\right)=5\pi.
$$

### Example 2: Equal Chords

A circle contains two chords of equal length. What can you conclude about the corresponding arcs?

Equal chords subtend equal arcs.

Therefore the arcs have equal measure.

### Example 3: Comparing Chords

Chord $AB$ subtends a central angle of $120^\circ$ while chord $CD$ subtends a central angle of $80^\circ$.

Which chord is longer?

Since

$$
120^\circ>80^\circ,
$$

arc $AB$ is larger than arc $CD$.

Therefore

$$
AB>CD.
$$

### Example 4: Perpendicular Radius

A radius is drawn perpendicular to a chord of length $12$.

Find the length of each half of the chord.

A radius perpendicular to a chord bisects the chord.

Thus each half has length

$$
\frac{12}{2}=6.
$$

### Example 5: Semicircle

A circle has radius $8$.

Find the length of a semicircular arc.

A semicircle corresponds to

$$
180^\circ=\pi \text{ radians}.
$$

Therefore

$$
s=r\theta=8\pi.
$$

Thus the arc length is

$$
\boxed{8\pi}.
$$

## Strategy Checklist

* Determine whether the problem is asking about arc measure or arc length.
* Convert degrees to radians before using $s=r\theta$.
* Look for equal chords and equal arcs.
* Compare arc measures when comparing chord lengths.
* Search for symmetry involving radii and chords.
* Remember that diameters create semicircles.

## Common Pitfalls

* Confusing arc measure with arc length.
* Using degrees directly in the formula $s=r\theta$.
* Assuming equal arcs imply equal lengths in different circles.
* Forgetting that a diameter is the longest chord.
* Missing the fact that a radius perpendicular to a chord bisects the chord.
